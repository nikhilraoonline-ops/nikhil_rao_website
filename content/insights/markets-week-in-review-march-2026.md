A choppy week across asset classes, characterised by intraday reversals and a cautious tone heading into month-end. Below is a structured recap of the key macro and market narratives from the week of March 24–28, 2026.

---

## The Week's Key Themes

1. **Tariff headlines returned** — renewed trade friction between the US and its trading partners weighed on risk sentiment mid-week
2. **Fed speakers reinforced patience** — multiple FOMC members pushed back against rate cut expectations, citing sticky services inflation
3. **Eurozone PMI flash contraction** — a surprise miss rattled EUR and European equities
4. **Commodities caught a bid** — gold hit fresh highs; oil steadied on supply discipline
5. **Month-end positioning** — fixed income flows and equity rebalancing added to intraday noise

## Equities: Risk-Off Mid-Week, Partial Recovery Thursday

US equities had a turbulent week. The **S&P 500** opened Monday near record territory but gave up ground sharply by Wednesday after the White House signalled new tariff measures on semiconductor imports — a sector that accounts for a significant share of S&P 500 earnings. By Thursday, a better-than-expected durable goods print and some short-covering drove a partial reversal.

**Weekly snapshot (approx.):**

| Index       | Friday Close | Week (approx.) |
|------------|-------------|----------------|
| S&P 500    | ~5,847       | -0.7%          |
| NASDAQ 100 | ~20,410      | -1.2%          |
| FTSE 100   | ~8,313       | +0.3%          |
| NIFTY 50   | ~22,147      | -0.5%          |
| DAX        | ~19,542      | -0.4%          |

Defensives outperformed — consumer staples and healthcare held up relative to technology, which bore the brunt of the tariff-linked semiconductor fears. The equal-weight S&P 500 outperformed the cap-weight index for the third week running, consistent with a rotation away from mega-cap technology.

**The structural point beneath the noise:** US equity valuations remain stretched relative to the risk-free rate. At a trailing P/E of ~22x on a 4.3% 10-year Treasury, the earnings yield premium over Treasuries is near its thinnest since 2007. This doesn't mean equities sell off tomorrow — but it does mean the index needs consistently positive earnings surprises to justify current prices.

## Fixed Income: Rates Range-Bound, Curve Watching the Fed

Treasuries traded in a narrow range for most of the week. The **10-year UST** opened around 4.32% and closed Friday at approximately 4.28% — a slight bull-flattening bias as growth concerns from the tariff headlines pulled long-end yields down modestly, while front-end rates stayed anchored to Fed speak.

Two themes dominated rates desks:

**1. The Fed's patience narrative**

Three FOMC members spoke publicly this week — Governor Waller, President Logan (Dallas), and President Collins (Boston). All three echoed the same message: the disinflation progress of 2024 has stalled, core PCE is still running above target, and the Committee sees no urgency to cut. Waller was the most explicit: *"I need several more months of favourable data before I'm comfortable supporting rate cuts."*

Markets are now pricing approximately 1.5 cuts for 2026, down from 2.5 cuts priced at the start of the year. The front end of the curve remains inverted — 2-year at 4.67% versus 10-year at 4.28% — though the inversion has narrowed from its peak of ~80bps.

**2. Duration management at month-end**

March 31 brings significant month-end duration extension for benchmarked fixed income funds. Bloomberg Aggregate benchmark duration is expected to extend ~0.06 years due to index rebalancing, which mechanically forces buying of long-end Treasury and agency MBS. This provided a technical tailwind for long-dated Treasuries late in the week that is largely independent of the fundamental picture.

## Eurozone: PMI Shock Hits EUR and Bunds

The week's biggest surprise came on Monday with the **Eurozone Composite PMI flash estimate** coming in at 49.1 — below the 50 contraction threshold and below the 51.0 consensus estimate. The miss was driven entirely by the services sector, which had been the last remaining source of momentum in European activity data.

The market reaction was swift:

- **EUR/USD** fell from 1.0890 to 1.0800 intraday, before recovering partly to close ~1.0842 on the week
- **German Bund 10-year yield** dropped ~8bps on the day (bond prices rose) as markets repriced a more aggressive ECB path
- **Euro Stoxx 50** fell 1.3% on Monday before partially recovering

The ECB is now widely expected to cut rates at its April meeting — the swap market is pricing ~85% probability of a 25bps cut, up from ~65% pre-PMI. The divergence between the Fed (on hold) and ECB (cutting) is now a key macro trade, and has been expressed through long EUR/USD put spreads and EUR rates receiver positions by macro funds.

This Atlantic momentum divergence — sluggish eurozone growth, resilient US labor market — has been building for months, but this week's PMI print crystallised it.

## FX: Dollar Strengthens, EM Currencies Under Pressure

The combination of Fed patience and eurozone weakness gave the **US Dollar** a strong week. The **DXY index** rose roughly 0.5% on the week, with gains concentrated against EUR and GBP.

**USD/INR** drifted higher to ~83.45, reflecting broader EM dollar strength rather than any India-specific news. The RBI's FX reserve buffer (currently estimated near $650bn) limits sharp INR depreciation, but the structural direction in a risk-off / dollar-strong environment is clear.

**GBP/USD** was relatively resilient, holding near 1.2634, as UK data remained messy but not dramatically worse. February retail sales came in roughly flat, and the Bank of England's last meeting (unchanged, 5–4 vote) kept GBP divergence from EUR/USD moderate.

**EM FX broadly** had a poor week. The tariff-headline-driven risk-off mid-week hit commodity-linked EM currencies (BRL, CLP, ZAR) harder than Asian EM. This pattern — US trade friction → commodity price uncertainty → commodity-exporting EM currencies weaken — is becoming a familiar transmission channel in 2026.

## Commodities: Gold at Fresh Highs, Oil Finds a Floor

**Gold** was the week's standout performer, reaching approximately **$2,648/oz** — a fresh all-time high in nominal terms. The drivers:

- Central bank buying remains robust; China and India remain consistent accumulators
- Dollar strength would normally suppress gold, but the geopolitical premium and de-dollarisation narrative continues to overwhelm the correlation at the margin
- Real yields remain in positive territory (10Y TIPS ~1.8%) but well below the levels that would classically justify selling gold

**WTI crude** settled near $71.24/bbl, finding support after an early-week dip to ~$69. OPEC+ supply discipline held — no indications of quotas being loosened ahead of the June meeting. The demand picture remains the uncertainty: China's state PMI (due in the first week of April) will be closely watched for signs of industrial demand recovery.

Conversely, a weak print (+100k or below) alongside any downward revision would re-open the 'growth scare' narrative that briefly surfaced in Q3 2025.

## A Brief Thought on the Macro Positioning for April

Risk assets look technically fragile heading into April — breadth indicators have been deteriorating even as index levels held up, and the VIX at ~17.8 doesn't scream panic but is no longer pricing complacency either.

The more interesting positioning risk may be in rates, not equities. Markets have been in a relatively narrow range (10-year between 4.20% and 4.50%) since November 2025. The absence of a clear catalyst — either deeply weak data to justify cuts, or a re-acceleration to justify pricing hikes — means rates could continue to drift range-bound well into Q2.

In that environment, carry strategies (long credit spreads, short vol) have tended to outperform directional bets. IG credit spreads remain tight at ~90bps OAS, offering limited cushion but reasonable current income if the range holds.
