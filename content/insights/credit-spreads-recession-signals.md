Equity investors tend to watch equities for signs of economic stress. But the bond market — specifically the credit market — frequently signals trouble first.

Credit spreads: the gap between what companies pay to borrow and what governments pay. When that gap widens, it means the market is demanding more compensation for lending to corporations — and historically, wide credit spreads are one of the clearest warning signs that a recession is approaching or already underway.

## What Is a Credit Spread?

A credit spread is the difference (in basis points) between the yield on a corporate bond and the yield on a comparable-maturity government bond (the "risk-free" rate):

$$\text{Spread} = Y_{corporate} - Y_{risk-free}$$

For a 10-year investment-grade (IG) corporate bond yielding 5.2% when the 10-year Treasury yields 4.3%, the credit spread is **90bps**.

The spread compensates investors for two things:
1. **Default risk**: The probability that the company won't repay the principal or interest
2. **Liquidity risk**: Corporate bonds are less liquid than Treasuries; investors want compensation for the possibility of not being able to sell easily

## High Yield vs. Investment Grade

Credit is typically segmented by quality:

| Segment          | Rating (S&P / Moody's)    | Typical Spread Range   |
|-----------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Investment Grade | BBB/Baa and above        | 50–200bps             |
| High Yield (HY)  | BB/Ba and below          | 200–600bps (normal)   |
| Distressed       | CCC and below            | 600bps+               |

**High yield (HY) spreads are the most recession-sensitive** because:
- HY issuers have weaker balance sheets and fewer refinancing options
- In a slowdown, their default probabilities jump non-linearly
- Institutional investors often have mandates that force selling when bonds cross quality thresholds

The ICE BofA US High Yield Index option-adjusted spread (OAS) is the most widely watched gauge.

## The Historical Record

The predictive power of credit spreads for recessions is well-documented:

**2007–2008 (GFC)**: HY spreads began widening in mid-2007 — well before the banking system crisis became apparent to most equity investors. By the peak (December 2008), HY OAS exceeded 2,000bps. The equity market trough came in March 2009.

**2020 (COVID)**: Spreads exploded from ~300bps to nearly 1,100bps in the span of three weeks (February–March 2020) — one of the fastest moves on record. Just as remarkable was the reversal: Fed intervention (buying corporate bonds through ETFs and the Primary Market Corporate Credit Facility) compressed spreads back to pre-COVID levels by mid-2021.

**2022 (Rate shock)**: Spreads widened modestly — HY OAS peaked around 580bps — but remained below the recessionary threshold. This was consistent with the eventual verdict: a slowdown, but not a recession.

## The Spread-Recession Rule of Thumb

Market practitioners often use the following qualitative thresholds for HY OAS:

- **< 300bps**: Risk-on, loose financial conditions, credit highly accessible
- **300–450bps**: Neutral; markets pricing some uncertainty
- **450–600bps**: Caution zone; tighter conditions, risk aversion building
- **> 600bps**: Recessionary signal; credit availability tightening materially

Note these are heuristics, not rules — every cycle has its own character.

## Why Equity Investors Should Watch Spreads

Credit markets lead equities for a structural reason: the capital structure priority.

Debt holders are paid before equity owners. When a company runs into financial stress:
- Credit holders sell first (tighter covenants, clearer early signals)
- Equity holders see the full downside last — but also face more dilution in distressed scenarios

This means **spread widening often precedes equity drawdowns** of the same company or sector.

In practice, watching the **ratio of HY spread widening to equity VIX** can give a useful relative read: if spreads are widening but VIX is low, equity markets may be complacent.

## The Fed Put and Its Credit Analogue

Post-GFC, central bank intervention has complicated the predictive value of spreads. The "Fed put" — the expectation that the Fed will cut rates or intervene to prevent a deep downturn — is mirrored in credit by the expectation of liquidity support.

The COVID episode tested this: the Fed's entry into corporate bond markets was unprecedented and massively compressed spreads. The result is that spreads may now understate true credit stress in tail-risk scenarios, because investors now price in central bank backstops.

## Bottom Line

Credit spreads remain one of the most information-dense signals available from public markets. They aggregate:
- Thousands of institutional investors' real-money views on corporate creditworthiness
- The current price of systemic risk
- Leading information about economic conditions ahead

For anyone building a macro view — whether for portfolio construction or simply for context — watching HY and IG spreads alongside equity and rates is not optional. It's the market giving you its honest opinion about what's coming.
