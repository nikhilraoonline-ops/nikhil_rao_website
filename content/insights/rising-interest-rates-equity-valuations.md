When central banks raise rates, the intuitive hit is on bonds: prices fall, yields rise. But equities feel the impact through a more distributed — and often more dangerous — set of channels.

## The DCF Framework: Rates as the Anchor of All Valuation

Most equity valuation, at its core, comes down to discounted cash flow. The intrinsic value of a stock is the present value of all future free cash flows, discounted at a rate that reflects the riskiness of those cash flows.

$$P = \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \frac{FCF_t}{(1 + r)^t}$$

Where **r** is the discount rate — typically the risk-free rate plus an equity risk premium. When the risk-free rate rises (as happens during a tightening cycle), **r** rises, and the denominator of every future cash flow grows. The present value falls.

This is not merely theoretical. In 2022, as the Federal Reserve hiked rates by 425bps in one of the fastest tightening cycles since the 1980s, the US equity market fell more than 20% peak-to-trough. Every 100bps rise in the discount rate has an outsized impact on stocks whose value is heavily weighted toward far-future cash flows — i.e., growth stocks.

## Why Growth Stocks Are More Sensitive

Consider two companies:

- **Company A** (value/income): Generates £100 in FCF this year, growing at 3% annually
- **Company B** (growth): Generates £10 in FCF this year, growing at 25% annually for 10 years

If the discount rate rises from 7% to 9%, Company A's intrinsic value might fall 15–20%. Company B might fall 35–45%, because the bulk of its value is in years 5–10 — those distant cash flows are discounted much more aggressively.

This duration-like sensitivity is why the 2022 sell-off was catastrophic for growth and technology names that had been bid up on near-zero discount rates in 2020–2021.

## The Equity Risk Premium Puzzle

Rates rising doesn't automatically mean equities are cheap on a relative basis. What matters is the **equity risk premium (ERP)** — the excess return investors demand over the risk-free rate for holding equities.

$$ERP = E[R_{equity}] - R_f$$

If rates rise but equities fall proportionally, the ERP remains stable. The concern arises when:

1. Rates rise fast (as in 2022), causing an immediate repricing
2. ERP *compresses* because investors chase yield in bonds — making equities relatively expensive again

The Fed Model — comparing the earnings yield of equities (E/P) to the 10-year Treasury yield — is a useful (if imperfect) heuristic. In late 2022, Treasury yields caught up to and briefly exceeded the S&P 500 earnings yield for the first time in over a decade, raising legitimate questions about relative attractiveness.

## Sector Implications

Not all sectors respond equally:

| Sector            | Rate Sensitivity | Rationale |
|------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Technology (Growth) | High (negative) | Long-duration cash flows |
| Real Estate (REITs) | High (negative) | Leverage + yield competition |
| Utilities          | High (negative) | Defensive yield play |
| Financials (Banks) | Positive         | Net interest margins expand |
| Energy             | Moderate (mixed) | Macro driver outweighs rates |
| Consumer Staples   | Low-moderate     | Pricing power offsets |

Banks are the classic rate beneficiary — their net interest income expands as lending rates rise faster than deposit rates (the "repricing gap"). This is why the financial sector often outperforms in early tightening cycles.

## What the 2022–2023 Cycle Taught Us

The cycle was unusual in several respects:

1. **Speed**: The Fed hiked by 75bps four consecutive times — the fastest tightening in decades
2. **Starting point**: Valuations were historically stretched, with many growth stocks at 30–100x revenue
3. **Earnings resilience**: Corporate earnings held up better than consensus expected, which partially buffered the multiple compression

The lesson for investors: **duration matters as much in equities as in bonds**. When rates normalize from zero, the longest-duration assets — speculative growth, pre-revenue tech, loss-making companies with a "story" — reprice most violently.

## Where Does That Leave Us?

As of early 2025, rates remain structurally higher than the 2010–2020 decade. The 10-year UST yield sits around 4.2–4.5%, compared to sub-2% for most of the last decade. This has two implications:

- **Valuation bars are higher**: A 20-25x P/E multiple for the S&P 500 is harder to justify when the risk-free rate is 4%+ versus 1%
- **Quality premium persists**: Cash-generative, margin-stable businesses trade at a structural premium to concept-stage companies that require capital

The era of "free money" artificially inflated multiples. The adjustment isn't over — but for investors who understand where the value sits in the discount rate framework, the environment is navigable.
